What “proximity to cancer research” means here

The question is not “is this a good scientist?” but “how directly does this person’s own research bear on cancer?” A world-class theoretical chemist whose work never touches oncology is, for this specific purpose, distant from cancer research — and the ranking says so plainly. The intent is to help a reader find the people at UNR Chemistry and BMB whose labs actually engage cancer biology or therapy.

The score (0–10)

Each faculty member receives points across five factors. The factors are additive and capped at 10. The same rubric is mirrored in assets/data.js (see RUBRIC) so the code and this page stay in sync.

The four tiers

TierMeaningTypical score
DirectCancer is an explicit subject of the lab — anticancer agents, tumor biology, chemoprevention, oncogenic signaling.7–10
Indirect / applicableThe lab builds methods, molecules, or studies pathways applied to cancer, but cancer is not its primary focus.3–5
TangentialOnly shared techniques or conserved biology connect the work to cancer; the lab pursues no cancer application itself.1–2.5
No connectionNo meaningful connection to cancer found in the public record.0–0.5

Scores are a structured editorial judgment, not a measurement. Two people one point apart are effectively tied; the tiers carry the real signal. Where a factor was ambiguous, the write-up on each card states the reasoning so a reader can disagree with an informed basis.

How faculty were selected

The pool is the research-active professorial faculty (assistant, associate, and full professors, plus research professors) listed on the two departmental directories as of July 2026. Purely teaching-track instructors, emeritus faculty, and administrative staff without an active research program were not scored. Both departments’ full directories are linked from the Sources page.

Student and trainee outcomes: the evidence bar

Tracking where a professor’s students went is the least-documented part of any such survey, and the easiest to get wrong. The rule used here is strict: a former student or postdoc is named only when a citable public source documents their destination — a lab’s own alumni page, a public professional profile, or a news item. Where nothing is published, the card says so rather than guessing. No names were inferred, and during research two plausible-looking matches were discarded after they turned out to be different people.

The badge “runs own lab” is applied only when a trainee holds a verified independent faculty appointment. Note that a well-documented trainee lineage is a property of a lab’s record-keeping, not of its cancer relevance: the fullest verified lineages here belong to the Tucker and Tal-Gan groups, which sit lower in the cancer ranking than several labs with no public alumni data at all.

Limitations

This is a snapshot of public information on a single date, assembled by an automated research process and open to error. Faculty pages are sometimes stale, cross-appointments blur department boundaries, and absence of evidence (especially for student outcomes) is not evidence of absence. Treat every entry as a pointer to the primary sources, which are all linked, rather than as a final word.
